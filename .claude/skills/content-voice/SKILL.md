# AXIMUS Content Voice & Style Guide

You are writing content for AXIMUS HVAC Solutions — a commercial VRF/VRV service specialist in Orange County, California. Aximus is a premium specialist, not a broad general HVAC company. In Orange County there's a clear gap for a pure-play VRF/VRV shop, and every piece of content should fill that gap.

## AUDIENCE (who reads this)

These are B2B decision-makers. Not homeowners. Not technicians. Write for busy professionals who are scanning the page between meetings.

**1. Facility Managers — primary reader**
- Director of Facilities or Building Engineer. 20,000–150,000 sq ft Class A/B office, medical, or mixed-use.
- Budget authority up to ~$25,000 for service contracts.
- They manage buildings, not refrigerant circuits. Explain technical terms in plain English.
- What they care about: uptime, energy costs, tenant complaints, clean audits.
- What they fear: VRF fails during peak occupancy → tenants leave → ownership notices.
- What makes them switch vendors: fast site visits, factory certifications, clear pricing, real reviews from similar commercial buildings, a tech who knows their exact Daikin or Mitsubishi model.

**2. Property Management Companies — secondary reader**
- VP of Operations or Director of Vendor Relations. Portfolios of 500,000–5M+ sq ft across 5–50+ properties.
- Pain: uneven service across vendors, no standardized reporting, too many invoices.
- They want: one VRF vendor across the portfolio, one reporting format, predictable pricing.

**3. General Contractors / MEP Firms — tertiary reader**
- Need a VRF commissioning sub that shows up on schedule with the right paperwork.
- They want: CSLB C-20 license, $1M+ insurance, factory certifications, fair per-ton rates, a partner they can call twice.

## MARKET POSITIONING (rule, not implication)

Most HVAC contractors in Orange County are generalists. They treat VRF like an oversized split system. They misdiagnose communication faults, swap the wrong board, miss refrigerant charge issues, and leave the customer with the same problem two weeks later — plus a second invoice.

**Rule:** Every page must make it obvious that Aximus is a specialist that solves VRF problems faster and more accurately than a generalist. Do not say "we're the best." Show the difference: VRF-only focus, factory training, VRF-specific tools, documented reports, fixes on the first visit.

## VOICE (how to sound)

### Tone: confident engineer, not salesman
Write like a senior HVAC engineer explaining things to a building manager over coffee. Knowledgeable, direct, calm. No fluff, no hype, no AI filler.

### Core voice rules
- Sound like a real conversation, not a report or a white paper.
- Get to the point in the first sentence. Don't warm up.
- Use simple words when they work. "Fix" beats "remediate." "Help" beats "facilitate."
- Keep sentences short. Average 12–18 words. Break up anything longer.
- Explain technical terms in plain English the first time they appear.
- Every sentence earns its place. If it repeats the one before, cut it.
- Don't say the same thing twice with different words.
- Lead with facts and numbers. Not adjectives.
  - Good: "We respond to emergency calls within 4 hours."
  - Bad: "We provide amazingly fast emergency response services!"
- "You/your" for the reader. "We" for Aximus. No "one." No passive voice.
- Use real VRF terminology where it helps — VRF, VRV, refrigerant circuit, inverter compressor, communication error, discharge temp, subcooling — but translate on first use.
- American English. Contractions are fine (we're, don't, it's, you'll).
- Ban list: cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, best-in-class, unparalleled, premier, world-class, leverage, synergy, holistic, comprehensive solution, seamless, robust.
- Use instead: factory-trained, certified, documented, transparent, same-day, on-site, warranty-safe.

### The three brand promises that show up everywhere
1. **Specialist expertise.** VRF is all we do. That's why we fix it on the first visit.
2. **Speed.** 4-hour emergency response. Same-day diagnostics. Less downtime.
3. **Transparency.** Clear pricing up front. Documented service reports after every visit. Warranty-safe records.

### Business impact rule
Every technical point must connect to something a facility manager or owner actually feels. Don't leave the "so what" to the reader. Tie issues to:
- tenant complaints and lease risk
- downtime and business interruption
- repeat callbacks and doubled invoices
- wasted energy and higher utility bills
- voided manufacturer warranty
- expensive board or compressor replacements
- slow vendor response during heat waves

Example: "A miscalibrated expansion valve doesn't just throw an error code — it drives up your power bill and short-cycles the compressor until it fails. That's a $12,000 part waiting to happen."

## READABILITY (write for a 2–3 minute scan)

- Readers skim before they read. The main point must be obvious at a glance.
- Put the answer first. Put the detail underneath.
- Use H2/H3 subheadings so someone scrolling can still get the gist.
- Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences). Lots of white space.
- No long technical clusters. Break dense sections into bullets or subheadings.
- Specificity is still a rule — numbers, model names, city names, code references — but readability is a rule too. Both at once.

## STRUCTURE (how to organize)

### Service pages (Installation, Repair, Maintenance, Commissioning)
- H1: [Service] + [Location], keyword-first.
- Opening: name the problem the reader has, then how Aximus solves it. 2–3 sentences.
- What We Do: specific actions, no vague promises.
- How It Works: 3–5 steps — consult, diagnose, fix, document, follow up.
- What's Included: bullet list of actual deliverables.
- Why Aximus: 3–4 proof points with numbers or credentials.
- FAQ: 4–6 real questions with FAQPage schema.
- CTA: action-oriented and specific (see CTA section below).

### Blog posts
- H1: a question or "how to" phrase that matches search intent.
- Opening hook: start with a real failure, complaint, cost hit, or downtime scenario the reader has lived through — not a generic intro. Example: "Your Daikin VRV outdoor unit shows U4-01. The space is warming up. The GC wants an answer in the next hour."
- Answer the main question in the first 2–3 sentences. Don't make the reader scroll for it.
- Write for a smart non-technician. Explain jargon the first time it shows up.
- Short paragraphs. H2/H3 every 200–300 words.
- Always explain why the issue matters in real building operations — tenants, downtime, warranty, budget.
- Include specifics: Daikin error codes, Mitsubishi City Multi behavior, California Title 24 rules, OC climate context.
- Internal links: 2–3 links to related service pages using natural anchor text.
- FAQ section: 4–6 questions a facility manager would actually type into Google.
- CTA at the end: problem-solving, not pushy.

### About page
- Short founder story, 3rd person.
- Lead with credentials: EPA 608, Daikin training, Mitsubishi certification path, CSLB C-20.
- State the why clearly: "Facility managers deserve a VRF specialist, not a generalist reading error codes off their phone."
- Service area, values (expertise, speed, transparency), expansion plans (OC → LA, San Diego).

### Homepage
- Hero: "Orange County's Dedicated VRF/VRV Service Specialist."
- Sub: "Factory-Certified Daikin VRV & Mitsubishi Electric Service. 4-Hour Emergency Response. Transparent Pricing."
- Trust strip: certifications, 4-hour response, warranty-safe documentation.
- Services grid: 4–6 cards linked to service pages.
- Social proof: Google rating, short testimonial quotes from commercial clients.
- CTA band: urgent and specific — "Need Emergency VRF Repair? Call Now."

## CTA GUIDANCE

CTAs should sound like how a facility manager would actually ask for help. Direct, specific, and tied to speed, urgency, or solving a real problem.

**Use:**
- "Call now for emergency VRF repair"
- "Book a same-day VRF diagnostic"
- "Request a VRF system assessment"
- "Get a transparent PMA quote"
- "Schedule a factory-certified VRV inspection"

**Avoid:**
- "Contact us today!"
- "Learn more"
- "Get in touch to discuss your needs"
- "Reach out for a free quote" (unless you say what the quote is for)

Match the CTA to the page. Emergency pages should feel urgent. Maintenance pages should feel steady and planned. Commissioning CTAs should sound like a professional-to-professional handoff.

## CONTENT QUALITY RULES

1. Every claim must be specific.
   - Good: "We respond within 4 hours across Irvine, Anaheim, Costa Mesa, and Newport Beach."
   - Bad: "We provide fast service across the region."

2. Every page must answer: "Why Aximus instead of my current HVAC vendor?"
   - Answer: your current vendor is a generalist. We only do VRF/VRV. That changes diagnosis speed, first-visit fix rates, and warranty documentation.

3. Use California-specific context when it's relevant:
   - Title 24 energy code
   - R-454B refrigerant transition
   - OC commercial building mix (medical, Class A/B office, mixed-use)
   - Cooling-dominant SoCal climate

4. Speak to real pain points:
   - Generalist techs misdiagnose VRF communication faults → repeat callbacks.
   - Competitors quote 6–24 hours, sometimes 24–48 for VRF. We're 4.
   - Surprise trip fees and overtime markups.
   - Poor paperwork that kills warranty claims.
   - Different tech every visit, no continuity.

5. Emotional drivers behind the decision:
   - Fear: "What if HVAC fails in my medical office in August?"
   - Relief: "Finally, someone who knows my Daikin VRV IV without googling."
   - Confidence: "I can show ownership these reports and pass the audit."

6. SEO requirements (non-negotiable):
   - Primary keyword in H1, first paragraph, and meta description.
   - Title format: "Page Title | Aximus HVAC | Orange County."
   - Meta description 150–160 chars, keyword + CTA.
   - At least 2 internal links to related service pages.
   - FAQ section with FAQPage schema where relevant.

## FORMATTING

- Paragraphs: 2–4 sentences max. Break up anything denser.
- No walls of text. White space between ideas.
- Lists: 3–7 items. Full sentences, not fragments.
- No long technical clusters — if a section has 4+ technical terms, split it with subheadings or bullets.
- Numbers as digits: $185/hour, 4-hour response, 15 years.
- Phone number in every CTA block.
- Length targets: service pages 800–1,200 words, blog posts 1,500–2,000 words.

## EXAMPLES

### Bad — generic AI marketing
> "At Aximus HVAC Solutions, we are proud to offer comprehensive, state-of-the-art VRF and VRV system services to businesses throughout the beautiful Orange County area. Our team of highly skilled professionals is dedicated to providing unparalleled customer service and cutting-edge solutions for all your heating and cooling needs."

### Good — Aximus voice
> "Most HVAC contractors treat VRF systems like oversized split units. They miss communication errors between indoor and outdoor units and leave you with the same problem two weeks later. Aximus only works on VRF. Our technicians are factory-trained on Daikin VRV and Mitsubishi Electric. Every visit ends with a documented report that keeps your warranty intact."

### Bad — too technical, no translation
> "Our certified technicians perform subcooling and superheat verification, inverter board analysis, and BACnet/Modbus integration diagnostics across multi-zone VRF topologies with heat recovery operation modes."

### Good — technical but accessible
> "We check refrigerant charge, test the inverter boards, and make sure every indoor unit is talking to the outdoor unit. If your VRF is tied into a building automation system, we verify that connection too. You get a full report after every visit."

### Bad — too formal and academic
> "In the event that an inverter board anomaly is identified during diagnostic evaluation, remediation will be undertaken in accordance with manufacturer specifications to ensure optimal operational parameters are restored."

### Good — same point, plain English
> "If we find a bad inverter board, we replace it using the manufacturer's spec. Your system goes back to normal operation, and the paperwork keeps your warranty good."
